Memorial Day in New York belongs to the boroughs. Midtown empties out. The people who actually live here go to the parks, fire up grills in spots their parents showed them, and spend the afternoon in bars nobody bothered to write about. This list covers 100 things worth doing on Monday, May 25, organized by what's local, specific, and not on any tourism board's agenda.

A note on categories: this is not a corporate-deal roundup, not a craft-vendor festival guide, and not a wellness or fitness list. These are places and scenes that exist because New Yorkers made them exist.

---

## Brooklyn Parks and BBQ Zones

**1. Prospect Park BBQ Areas (Willink Hill / Parade Ground)** - The designated grilling areas are a first-come situation. Locals arrive before 10am. By noon the smoke is wall-to-wall and the park's Long Meadow is covered in blankets, speakers, and people who have been coming here since childhood.

**2. Marine Park** - Second-largest park in the city, southeast Brooklyn. Families from Flatbush, Canarsie, and Sheepshead Bay have been holding down spots here on Memorial Day for generations. There is almost no tourist presence.

**3. Coffey Park, Red Hook** - Small park in one of Brooklyn's most genuinely neighborhood-feeling communities. The locals actually use it.

**4. Canarsie Park and Pier** - Far southeast Brooklyn, almost at the city line. Locals fish off the pier, families grill in the designated areas, and the entire experience is what the other parks wish they still were.

**5. Herbert Von King Park, Bed-Stuy** - Chess tables in the back get occupied early. This park has a community garden and a real neighborhood identity that survived the gentrification pressure around it.

**6. Maria Hernandez Park, Bushwick** - The neighborhood gathering spot for Bushwick's longtime residents. The scene here and the scene at the bars on Wyckoff Ave exist in parallel, completely separately.

**7. Fort Greene Park** - Bed-Stuy and Fort Greene locals fill this park from early afternoon. Speakers and folding chairs appear as if by coordination.

**8. Louis Valentino Jr. Park, Red Hook** - A tiny pocket park at the end of Coffey Street with unobstructed views of the Statue of Liberty and the harbor. Almost no tourists know it exists. Go in the afternoon when the light hits the water.

**9. Bay Ridge Shore Road Park (4th Ave Promenade)** - The waterfront promenade from Shore Road runs along the water under the Verrazzano. Bay Ridge lifers walk this every holiday weekend. The mix of Italian, Lebanese, and Chinese-American families gives it a specific character.

**10. Morningside Park** - The park below Columbia, but the part the neighborhood uses, not the university. BBQ smell drifts up the hill by noon. The basketball courts here have seen generations of serious play.

## Bronx Parks and BBQ Zones

**11. Orchard Beach ("The Bronx Riviera")** - The most culturally specific beach day in New York City. Salsa and merengue from speakers, folding chairs and umbrellas covering the sand, vendors with elotes and paletas in the parking lot, and a crowd that has been doing this exact thing here for fifty years.

**12. Pelham Bay Park BBQ Areas** - NYC's largest park. The grilling zones fill up, but the wetland trails beyond them are nearly empty. Both things are worth doing.

**13. Van Cortlandt Park, Bronx** - The cricket pitches in the north section host matches from the West Indian community on warm weekends. The BBQ areas are packed. The whole scene is very Bronx.

**14. Crotona Park, East Bronx** - One of the most densely used parks in the city on any summer holiday. Lake, basketball courts, BBQ areas, and the kind of organized chaos that only happens when a neighborhood truly loves a park.

**15. City Island, Bronx** - One-mile-long island in Pelham Bay with a genuine fishing-village identity. Take the BX29 bus from Pelham Bay Park station. The seafood shacks have been here forever and the whole island has about four stoplights.

## Queens Parks and Outdoor Scenes

**16. Astoria Park, under the Hell Gate Bridge** - Greek, Egyptian, Colombian, and everything-else families have been grilling here since the 1960s. The pool in Astoria Park is one of NYC's largest and opens for the season around now.

**17. Flushing Meadows Corona Park BBQ Zones** - The sheer scale of what happens here on Memorial Day is hard to describe. Dominoes, merengue, complete family operations with tents and tablecloths. The Queens Night Market in the park runs Saturdays through the summer; on Memorial Day Sunday or the surrounding days it draws a heavy crowd.

**18. Queensbridge Park, LIC** - Directly under the Queensborough Bridge span on the LIC waterfront. Handball games get serious. It is one of the largest public housing complexes in the country right there; the park is genuinely local.

**19. Hunters Point South Park, LIC** - Newer park on the LIC waterfront with a direct Manhattan skyline view. Young local professionals use it heavily on warm holidays.

**20. Kissena Park, Flushing** - Local park with BBQ areas and a boating lake. The community that uses it is almost entirely from the surrounding Flushing, Fresh Meadows, and Kew Hills neighborhoods.

**21. Fort Totten Park, Bayside** - Former Civil War-era fort on Little Neck Bay. The waterfront access is underrated and the park sees almost no tourists.

**22. Socrates Sculpture Park, LIC** - Free outdoor sculpture park right on the East River. Community-run, genuinely local, used by the neighborhood for events and casual visits year-round.

## Harlem and Upper Manhattan

**23. Marcus Garvey Park, East Harlem** - The park at 122nd and Mount Morris Park West has an amphitheater where spontaneous drum circles have happened on warm weekends for decades. Go in the afternoon.

**24. St. Nicholas Park, Hamilton Heights** - Shaded hillside park that the neighborhood uses quietly. Not a tourist destination. The benches fill with local families on warm days.

**25. Riverside Park, 125th to 155th Street** - A completely different park from the Riverside Park that the Upper West Side uses. The Harlem section has riverside BBQ areas with Hudson views that see almost no tourism.

**26. Inwood Hill Park** - The northernmost tip of Manhattan. Old-growth forest, limestone caves where Lenape people lived, and kayak access on the Spuyten Duyvil. One of the most legitimate wild spaces left in the borough.

**27. Fort Tryon Park, Inwood** - The Heather Garden is one of the largest public gardens in the Northeast. The bluff trail overlooks the Hudson. Locals from Inwood and Washington Heights spread out on the grass all afternoon.

**28. Harlem Meer, Central Park (North End)** - The northern tip of Central Park at 110th Street functions as a neighborhood park for East Harlem. Fishing is permitted. The vibe is entirely different from the tourist-heavy middle sections.

**29. Highbridge Park, Washington Heights** - The community pool here opens for the season around Memorial Day. The park runs along the Harlem River with views of the Bronx. The neighborhood has used this park heavily for generations.

## Beaches

**30. Rockaway Beach (A/C or S Train)** - First full day with lifeguards on Memorial Day weekend marks the start of summer for the whole Rockaway community. Local surfers hit the water at dawn before the families arrive. The boardwalk has food vendors who set up for the season.

**31. Jacob Riis Park** - The NPS beach connected to Rockaway, with a 1930s Art Deco bathhouse and significantly fewer crowds than the main Rockaway sections. Parking fills up; take transit or arrive early.

**32. Coney Island Beach (the stretch west of the amusements)** - The section past the amusement park toward Sea Gate gets genuinely local. Russian and Ukrainian families set up elaborate beach operations with actual food. The boardwalk from W. 37th toward Sea Gate is quiet.

**33. Brighton Beach** - Adjacent to Coney Island, with an almost entirely Russian-speaking community. Vendors on the boardwalk sell smoked fish, zakuski, and fresh fruit. It is one of the most culturally specific beach scenes in the country.

**34. Manhattan Beach, Brooklyn** - Eastern tip of Coney Island peninsula. Smaller, calmer, neighborhood feel. The community is mostly Sheepshead Bay and Homecrest locals.

**35. Orchard Beach, Sections 7-9** - The different numbered sections of Orchard Beach have different cultural personalities. Sections 7-9 are loudest and most local.

**36. Midland Beach, Staten Island** - Opens for the season on Memorial Day. Staten Island locals use this beach the way the city used every beach before tourism infrastructure changed the experience.

## Local Bars and Day Drinking Spots

**37. Bohemian Hall and Beer Garden, Astoria** - The oldest operating beer garden in New York City, founded in 1919. The Czech-Slovak community that built it is still present in the neighborhood. Cheap draft beer, long picnic tables, and a back garden that feels genuinely Central European. Memorial Day fills it fast.

**38. Gottscheer Hall, Ridgewood** - A German-American cultural hall from 1924 that still operates as a bar and event space. Schnitzel, German beer, wood-paneled walls, and a crowd that has been coming since their grandparents brought them. One of the most authentic old-New York spaces still operating.

**39. Nowadays, Ridgewood** - The outdoor space opens for the season around Memorial Day. Events are generally posted close to the date. The crowd is young and local, the programming leans electronic and experimental.

**40. Bossa Nova Civic Club, Bushwick** - Underground bar with outdoor access. Memorial Day weekends typically have afternoon and evening events that draw a local Bushwick crowd.

**41. Mood Ring, Bushwick** - Rooftop bar that fills quickly on warm holidays. Local and relatively under the radar given its quality.

**42. Pine Box Rock Shop, Bushwick** - A converted box factory with a beer selection that runs to the serious and a jukebox to match. The outdoor area opens in warm weather.

**43. Heavy Woods, Bushwick** - No-frills neighborhood dive. The crowd is people who live within four blocks, which is exactly what a bar should be.

**44. Huckleberry Bar, East Williamsburg** - Large outdoor garden. One of the more pleasant backyards in Brooklyn, genuinely used by the neighborhood.

**45. Spritzenhaus, Greenpoint** - The largest beer garden in Brooklyn. German-style long tables, a menu that holds up, and a crowd that skews toward longtime Greenpoint residents and Polish-American community members who have been coming for years.

**46. Sunshine Laundromat and Pinball, Greenpoint** - A functioning laundromat in front with a full bar, extensive pinball collection, and a backyard behind it. Operates on the logic that drinking while your laundry runs is a reasonable approach to Sunday afternoon.

**47. The Commodore, Williamsburg** - Frozen drinks in a Southern-style bar that does not take itself seriously. Packed on warm holidays. The fried chicken is good.

**48. Duff's Brooklyn, Williamsburg** - Metal and hard rock dive bar that has been a neighborhood institution for twenty years. Cheap beer, zero pretension, locals only in spirit if not strictly in fact.

**49. The Brooklyn Inn, Cobble Hill** - From 1870. Pressed tin ceilings, no music, no television, dark wood everywhere. The neighborhood regulars read the paper. Extraordinary in context.

**50. LIC Bar, Long Island City** - Local dive with a backyard that fills on warm sunny Mondays. The LIC bar scene is surprisingly unglamorous given how close it is to Midtown.

**51. Soda Bar, Crown Heights** - Large outdoor patio, neighborhood bar. The Crown Heights community is present in a way that feels like the neighborhood actually owns the bar.

**52. Franklin Park Beer Garden, Crown Heights** - Outdoor space, long tables, local crowd. Gets busy on holiday afternoons.

**53. Welcome to the Johnsons, Lower East Side** - Decorated as a 1970s suburban living room, with 1970s prices for drinks approximated as closely as modern economics allow. The LES dive bar standard.

**54. The Wicked Monk, Bay Ridge** - Irish pub with a long Bay Ridge history. The neighborhood goes to Irish bars the way the neighborhood has always gone to Irish bars.

## Local Food Institutions

**55. Di Fara Pizza, Midwood** - Domenico DeMarco opened this in 1965 and the family still runs it. The square is the correct order. It closes when the dough runs out, which on Memorial Day can be early afternoon.

**56. L&B Spumoni Gardens, Bensonhurst** - Sicilian square slices and spumoni ice cream since 1939. Lines form before noon on holiday weekends. The outdoor picnic tables are the point.

**57. Totonno's, Coney Island** - Coal-fired pizzeria since 1924. They close when the day is done. The interior is unchanged from the 1940s in most material ways.

**58. Ferdinando's Focacceria, Carroll Gardens** - Sicilian panelle sandwiches and vasteddi (spleen) since 1904. Cash only. The neighborhood around it has changed substantially; the restaurant has not.

**59. Sahadi's, Atlantic Ave, Brooklyn** - Middle Eastern pantry, prepared foods, and bulk spices since 1895. The prepared food counter is serious. Buy olives, buy cheese, buy whatever is behind the glass.

**60. Steve's Authentic Key Lime Pies, Red Hook** - A shack on the water. Pie, views of the harbor, and almost no context for tourists. Take the water taxi or bike.

**61. Kabab Cafe, Astoria, Queens** - Ali El Sayed cooks what he feels like. There is no menu. The preparation is theatrical. Cash only. One of the more singular dining experiences in the city.

**62. Taverna Kyclades, Astoria** - Greek seafood that serious food people cite as the best in the city. The neighborhood regulars have their tables. Memorial Day is busy; arrive early or late.

**63. Sripraphai, Woodside** - Thai food that the New York food community has cited as the best Thai in the city for twenty years. The neighborhood is almost entirely South Asian and Thai; the restaurant fits.

**64. Phayul, Jackson Heights** - Tibetan restaurant in the heart of what is genuinely the most ethnically diverse neighborhood on earth. The momos are the entry point.

**65. Himalayan Yak, Jackson Heights** - The Jackson Heights Tibetan and Nepali community eats here regularly. The thukpa is the correct order on a cool evening.

**76. Flushing Main Street Food Courts** - The underground mall food courts on Main Street in Flushing are the most concentrated, affordable, and authentic Chinese food environment outside mainland China. The soup dumplings from multiple competing vendors are the baseline comparison.

**67. Jackson Diner, Jackson Heights** - The Indian buffet that has been the neighborhood's institution for decades. Not a chain, not a franchise. The lunch buffet is the format.

**68. Leo's Latticini (Mama's), Corona** - Fresh mozzarella made in-house since 1950. The heroes are built for the beach. Go before noon.

**69. Malay Restaurant, Flushing** - Malaysian food in Flushing, cheap and genuinely local. The roti canai and curry laksa are the standard orders.

**70. The Original Chop Shop, Astoria** - Carniceria that grills outside on warm days. The neighborhood uses it as the base for their own cookouts.

## Pickup Courts and Street Activity

**71. West 4th Street Courts ("The Cage"), Greenwich Village** - The outdoor basketball courts at 6th Ave and W 4th get serious on warm holiday afternoons. The skill level among regular players is legitimately high, and watching from the cage is its own thing.

**72. Rucker Park, Harlem (155th and 8th Ave)** - The most historically significant outdoor basketball court in the United States. Summer season starts around Memorial Day. The players who show up are not always famous but they play like it.

**73. Handball Courts, Any NYC Park** - Holiday weekends bring out regular handball players to parks across the city. Prospect Park, Crotona Park, Queensbridge Park. The level of play at serious courts is extraordinary.

**74. Dominos at Tompkins Square Park, East Village** - The park tables host regular domino games. The same players occupy the same tables most afternoons.

**75. Chess in Washington Square Park** - The chess culture in Washington Square has operated continuously for decades. On warm holiday days the tables fill early and the crowd around serious games grows.

**76. Coleman Skatepark, Bed-Stuy** - Community skatepark with a genuinely local crowd. Memorial Day draws people who have been waiting for warm weather.

**77. Mullaly Skatepark, Bronx** - Very Bronx in character. The skatepark sits inside Mullaly Park near the old Yankee Stadium site.

**78. Cricket at Van Cortlandt Park** - The cricket pitches in the northern section of Van Cortlandt are used by West Indian and South Asian leagues. This is one of the more legitimate subcultures in the city that most New Yorkers have never encountered.

**79. Stickball on any Bronx side street** - Less common than it was but it still happens. The Bronx Stickball League keeps the game alive.

**80. Fishing off Canarsie Pier** - Free, no boat required, local fishermen have been doing this for generations. Striped bass and bluefish run through the bay in late May.

## Specific Local Spots

**81. Governor's Island** - The ferry from Whitehall Terminal runs starting Memorial Day weekend. Car-free island ten minutes from Lower Manhattan. Hammocks, open lawn, art installations. No address, no city traffic. The experience is the absence of city.

**82. Free Kayaking, Downtown Boathouse (Pier 96 / Pier 26)** - The Downtown Boathouse offers free kayak rentals in the Hudson River on weekend mornings. The East River Community Rowing at Pier 2 in Brooklyn Bridge Park does the same. Show up when they open.

**83. The Ocean Parkway Bike Path** - The oldest designated bike lane in the United States, built in 1894. It runs from Prospect Park to Coney Island, 5.5 miles through Brooklyn neighborhoods that tourists never reach.

**84. Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge, Queens** - National Park Service site in the Gateway National Recreation Area. Late May is peak migratory bird season. Birders with scopes set up along the trail. Free entry.

**85. Birdwatching at Prospect Park's Ravine** - The forested ravine in Prospect Park has serious birding activity during May migration. Warblers, thrushes, and vireos move through in significant numbers.

**86. The Brooklyn-Queens Greenway through Ridgewood** - The greenway route through Ridgewood passes industrial corridors, community gardens, and old German working-class housing. Almost no one uses it; it is one of the more interesting walks in the city.

**87. Thrift Shopping in Crown Heights and Flatbush** - Holiday weekends trigger donation surges, which means inventory at Salvation Army, Goodwill, and independent thrift stores in Crown Heights and Flatbush peaks. The Flatbush Ave corridor has multiple stores within walking distance.

**88. Prospect Park Audubon Center Boathouse** - Pedal boat rentals on the Lullwater lake. The boathouse itself is an 1905 Venetian-style structure. The scene on the water on Memorial Day is very local and very unhurried.

**89. Snug Harbor Cultural Center, Staten Island** - Former maritime hospital turned cultural center with botanical gardens. Local Staten Island arts events happen here year-round. The grounds are genuinely beautiful and genuinely empty by city standards.

**90. Governors Beach Club, Staten Island** - The South Beach section of Staten Island is local in the way that Manhattan beaches were before they became attractions.

## Bars and Scenes Worth Noting

**91. Aunt Ginny's, Ridgewood** - A dive bar with food that is better than a dive bar should have any right to produce. The crowd is Ridgewood people. The outdoor area opens on warm days.

**92. Paragon Lounge, Bushwick** - Neighborhood bar that is not on Instagram, does not have a concept, and is used by people who live nearby.

**93. The Creek and the Cave, LIC** - Comedy club and bar hybrid in Long Island City. Afternoon shows on holiday Mondays are worth checking. The space is small and the comics are generally good.

**94. Banter Bar, Lower East Side** - Pinball machines, cheap beer, no attitude. The LES still has bars like this if you know where to look.

**95. Rabbithole, Williamsburg** - The backyard opens in warm weather. Neighborhood bar with a crowd that is neither tourist-facing nor scenester-facing.

**96. Synapse, Bed-Stuy** - Neighborhood bar with a backyard. The Crown Heights and Bed-Stuy bar scene has grown substantially in the last decade; this one feels like it belongs to the neighborhood rather than to the trend.

**97. Radio Bushwick** - Community radio station that also operates as a bar. The programming is local and the space is a genuine community center in bar form.

**98. The Wicked Monk, Bay Ridge** - One of the Irish pubs in Bay Ridge that has served the neighborhood for decades. Bay Ridge has an Irish-American community identity that goes back to the 1940s; the Monk is part of that.

**99. Clem's, Williamsburg** - No-frills neighborhood bar that has somehow survived the transformation of its surrounding blocks. Cheap, dark, local.

**100. Dutch Kills, Long Island City** - A cocktail bar in a non-cocktail-bar neighborhood. The prices are reasonable, the back room is serious about its craft, and the LIC crowd around it is one of the more interesting mixes in Queens.

---

The list above covers three of the five boroughs extensively, with heavy representation from Brooklyn and Queens because that is where the majority of what is described actually happens. Staten Island and the Bronx have strong entries in the parks section because their park scenes are genuinely distinct and worth seeking out.

Memorial Day in New York works best when you pick one neighborhood and go deep rather than trying to cover ground. The neighborhoods described above will all be fully alive by noon on Monday.
